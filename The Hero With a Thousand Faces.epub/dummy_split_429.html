Radioff, Proben der Volksliteratur der türkischen Stämme Süd-Siberiens (St. Petersburg, 1866-70), Vol. I, p. 285.—Breaking free from cosmogonic associations, the negative, clown-devil aspect of the demiurgic power has become a great favorite in the tales told for amusement. A vivid example is Coyote of the American plains. Reynard the Fox is a European incarnation of this figure.

1 The Kalevala (“The Land of Heroes”) in its present form is the work of Elias Lonnrot (1802-1884), a country physician and student of Finnish philology. Having collected a considerable body of folk poetry around the legendary heroes, Vainamoinen, Ilmarinen, Lemminkainen, and Kullervo, he composed these in co-ordinated sequence and cast them in a uniform verse (1835, 1849). The work comes to some 23,000 lines.
